The history of new media art

Art fell in love with the machine.
The art world wasn't ready.

From self-destructing sculptures in 1960 to glitchy internet art in 1997 — this is the 40-year story of artists who refused to wait for permission to use new technology.

Scroll to begin

The short version

Here's the thing nobody tells you about technology and art: they've been trying to date since the 1960s, and it almost never works out.

Every decade, a new wave of artists grabs whatever tech is lying around — mainframes, satellites, robots, the early internet — and tries to make it part of the art world. And almost every time, the art world says interesting, but not for us and quietly closes the door.

So the artists do something else: they build their own world. Their own galleries, their own festivals, their own museums, their own rules. Decade after decade, this happens again — until "New Media Art" stops being a phase and becomes its own entire universe, running in parallel to the art world everyone already knows.

This is that story. Four decades, four very different attempts to make tech and art work — and what happened each time they tried.

It starts, like most love stories, with someone walking into a lab.

1960 – 1969

When artists and engineers fell in love

Picture a Bell Labs engineer in 1962, alone at a mainframe the size of a room, making abstract art on a machine built for missile calculations. Nobody asked him to. He just wanted to see what would happen.

That's the sixties in a sentence: a brief, electric moment when artists, scientists, and engineers all wanted the same thing — to see what art and technology could do together — and nobody told them to stop.

Sculptures that destroyed themselves. Robots that responded to your voice. A gallery in London that put a computer next to a Bridget Riley painting like it was the most normal thing in the world. For about ten years, the art world was wide open to anyone curious enough to plug something in.

It wouldn't last. But while it did, it changed everything that came after.

Here's where it happened — click any moment to see it.

1962 — The first computer art

An engineer at Bell Labs named A. Michael Noll starts making abstract images with a mainframe — using math, not a paintbrush. It's clumsy, it's experimental, and it's the literal birth of computer art.

1966 — Artists hijack engineering

Billy Klüver, an engineer who fell in love with the art world, organizes 9 Evenings: Theatre and Engineering — ten artists, thirty engineers, one wild week of performances nobody had ever seen before.

1968 — The show that proved it could work

Cybernetic Serendipity opens in London — part art exhibit, part science fair, part fever dream. Robots, computer-generated poetry, and painting machines, all under one roof. It's still considered one of the most important tech-art shows ever staged.

1970 — Art and tech get an HQ

E.A.T. (Experiments in Art and Technology) — the group Klüver founded — builds the Pepsi Pavilion for Expo '70 in Osaka: an immersive dome a decade ahead of its time. This is basically virtual reality, in 1970.

1970 – 1979

The art world closed the door. So they built a basement.

The sixties ended with a hangover. The Vietnam War made "technology" a dirty word in art circles — Richard Serra literally said tech was tied to violence abroad and at home. Computers were still expensive, bulky, and impossible to access unless you worked at a university or a lab. The party was over.

But here's what's interesting: the work didn't stop. It just went underground.

While the official art world moved on, a strange, scrappy mix of engineers, hackers, musicians, and dropouts kept experimenting — usually after-hours, usually uninvited, usually for free. A NASA-trained scientist made music with a homemade computer and ended up jamming with the Grateful Dead. A robot learned to respond to human voices and movement, funded by a lighting company. An artificial intelligence taught itself to paint, decades before "AI art" was a phrase anyone used.

None of this happened in galleries. It happened in labs, basements, and university departments most art critics never set foot in.

This is where the underground decade actually happened.

1971 — The internet is born

Computers start talking to each other for the first time, thanks to a new shared protocol. Nobody knows it yet, but this is the plumbing that every future internet artwork will run through.

1973 — A machine learns to paint

Artist Harold Cohen starts building AARON, a program designed to make its own artistic decisions. It's one of the earliest serious attempts at what we'd now call AI-generated art — 50 years before the term went mainstream.

1974 — A robot that notices you

In London, engineer Edward Ihnatowicz finishes The Senster — a giant aluminum sculpture, funded by Philips, that physically reacts to people's voices and movement. It's part robot, part artwork, and entirely ahead of its time.

1977 — Apple II hits homes

Personal computers start showing up in regular households for the first time. This is the quiet moment that makes everything in the next two decades possible — art included.

1980 – 1989

Before the internet, they invented video calls anyway

Forget what you think you know about the eighties. While everyone remembers neon and synth-pop, a small group of artists were quietly inventing things we now take completely for granted — decades before the technology to do it properly even existed.

In 1980, two artists connected two cities — New York and Los Angeles — with a live video feed on giant public screens. Strangers three thousand miles apart could see each other, wave, dance, flirt. People on the sidewalk had no idea it was art. It's since been called "the mother of all video chats." This was 14 years before anyone had a webcam at home.

Four years later, Nam June Paik beamed a satellite broadcast of live art performances into homes around the world — ten million people watched. No livestream, no internet, just sheer audacity and a satellite link.

This was also the decade New Media Art finally got a permanent home: a festival in a small Austrian city called Linz. It wasn't fancy. It started as a music symposium. But Ars Electronica would go on to become the single most important meeting point this art world ever had — and it's still running today.

Meanwhile, Macintosh computers brought the mouse and the graphic interface into homes, arcade games turned into a billion-dollar industry, and a strange new subculture — cyberpunk — started imagining the future this was all building toward.

Here's how the decade unfolded.

1980 — The first video call (sort of)

Hole-in-Space connects strangers in New York and LA through live video on public screens — no warning, no explanation. People just start talking to each other across the country, live, for the first time ever.

1979 — A festival begins, almost by accident

What starts as a small Austrian music symposium becomes Ars Electronica — the festival that will define New Media Art for the next 40+ years and is still running today.

1984 — Ten million people watch a satellite artwork

Nam June Paik's Good Morning Mr. Orwell broadcasts live performances via satellite to audiences across continents — one of the largest audiences any artwork has ever reached in real time.

1984 — The Macintosh changes everything

Apple releases the Macintosh: mouse, graphic interface, the first computer that didn't require you to be an engineer to use it. This is the moment "using a computer" stops being a specialist skill.

1989 – 1997

They finally got their own museum. Then everything changed again.

1989 was a turning point — and not just for New Media Art. The Berlin Wall fell. The Soviet Union began to collapse. And in Germany, a new kind of institution was being built: ZKM, a museum designed specifically for media art, the first of its kind anywhere in the world.

For the first time in 30 years, this art finally had a real home. Museums. Festivals. Research labs. A whole support system, built from scratch, spreading across Europe — and, after the fall of communism, into Eastern Europe too, where artists who'd been working in squats and private apartments suddenly had access to actual institutions for the first time.

It looked like the happy ending. It wasn't quite.

Because while all this was being built, something else was happening completely outside of it: the World Wide Web. And once ordinary people could get online — no engineering degree required — an entirely new kind of artist showed up. Painters, photographers, filmmakers who'd never touched a mainframe in their life started making art on the internet itself. They called it Net Art, and it had almost nothing to do with the museums, labs, and festivals that had just spent a decade building themselves a home.

In 1997, both worlds ended up on the same stage at an awards ceremony — a Net Art collective in matching orange jackets, standing next to a Pixar director, both winners, both completely unsure what they had in common. That photo says everything: New Media Art had finally arrived. And it had just split in two.

Here's how it all came together — and split apart.

1989 — The first museum built for this art

ZKM opens in Germany — the first major museum in the world built specifically around media art. After 30 years of being the art world's outsider, this is the moment New Media Art gets an actual address.

1994 — The web goes public

Mosaic, the first real web browser, launches — and for the first time, getting online doesn't require a computer science degree. This single moment makes the next wave of art possible.

1996 — Net Art wins its first major award

Swiss collective etoy wins a top prize at Ars Electronica for Digital Hijack — a stunt that redirected hundreds of thousands of internet users to their site. This is hacking, performance, and art, all at once.

1997 — Two art worlds, one awkward photo

At the same award ceremony, a Net Art collective and a major animation studio director share the stage as fellow winners. Nobody in that photo is quite sure what they have in common — and that's the whole point.

1997 — and beyond

This story didn't end. You're standing inside its sequel.

Here's the part most people miss: New Media Art never went away after 1997. It just kept doing exactly what it had always done — quietly building things the mainstream wasn't ready for yet.

The glitchy, DIY internet art of the late nineties? That's the direct ancestor of meme culture, GIF art, and half of what's on your social media feed right now. The artists experimenting with AI-generated paintings in the 1970s? That conversation is happening again today, just with better tools and a much bigger audience. The telepresence experiments — people connecting live across cities through screens? You did that this morning, probably before breakfast.

Every time you've seen "new" technology collide with art — NFTs, AI image generators, VR galleries, interactive installations — you've actually been watching this same 60-year pattern repeat itself. Someone gets curious. The mainstream hesitates. The artists build it anyway.

That's the real takeaway here: this was never really a "history" chapter. It's a pattern that's still running, right now, and you're living inside the next chapter of it.

So — what will you build before someone tells you it's not allowed?

Based on "A Brief History of the New Media Art World," from Beyond New Media Art by Domenico Quaranta (Link Editions, 2013).
